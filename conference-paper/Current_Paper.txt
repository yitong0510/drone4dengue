Abstract 
Dengue fever remains a critical public health challenge, requiring accurate, localized early warning systems to mitigate outbreaks. Traditional prediction models often struggle to capture the complex, non-linear interactions between weather patterns, urban density, and historical transmission rates. This paper presents a robust machine learning pipeline for dengue case prediction utilizing historical surveillance data. We propose a stacking ensemble architecture combining Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) and Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM), optimized with Poisson and Tweedie objectives to handle the over-dispersed, count-based nature of epidemiological data. The methodology integrates automated daily data ingestion, deduplication, and reverse geocoding to extract granular spatial features. Furthermore, we engineer advanced temporal and biological features, including rolling weather lags, exponential weighted moving averages, and the Brière thermal performance curve for mosquito development. To capture spatial dynamics, K-Means and DBSCAN clustering are applied to differentiate urban and rural transmission patterns. Evaluated using a strict time-series split, the proposed stacking ensemble demonstrates superior predictive performance compared to standalone tree-based models. This framework provides a scalable, automated approach for public health authorities to anticipate localized dengue hotspots and deploy targeted interventions.

1	Introduction
Dengue fever is a rapidly spreading mosquito-borne viral disease that poses a significant global public health threat, particularly in tropical and subtropical regions. The incidence of dengue has grown dramatically in recent decades, driven by factors such as rapid urbanization, climate change, and increased global travel. Because there is currently no highly efficacious universal vaccine or specific antiviral treatment, public health strategies rely heavily on vector control and proactive outbreak management. However, deploying these interventions effectively requires accurate, highly localized early warning systems capable of predicting where and when outbreaks will occur.
Predicting localized dengue transmission is an inherently complex challenge. The life cycle of the primary vector, the Aedes aegypti mosquito, and the incubation period of the virus are highly sensitive to meteorological variables, particularly temperature, humidity, and rainfall. Furthermore, transmission dynamics are heavily influenced by spatial factors, such as urban population density, and historical infection rates within a community. Traditional epidemiological models often struggle to capture these non-linear, interacting variables, particularly when dealing with the over-dispersed, count-based nature of active case data.
Recent advancements in machine learning, particularly gradient boosting frameworks, have shown immense promise in epidemiological forecasting. While standalone models can capture complex relationships, they often fall short when predicting extreme outbreak events (hotspots) versus baseline endemic transmission.
In this paper we propose a comprehensive, automated machine learning pipeline for localized dengue case prediction. Our approach leverages historical surveillance data, enriched with granular spatial features extracted via reverse geocoding, and advances biological features such as Brière thermal performance curve. We introduce a  Stacking Ensemble architecture that combines Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) and Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM). By utilizing Poisson and Tweedie objective functions tailored for count data, and combining the base estimators with a Ridge Regression meta-learner, our model aims to provide superior predictive accuracy for active dengue cases. This framework is designed to serve as a scalable tool for authorities to anticipate hotspots.
Recent literature has increasingly shifted from traditional statistical models (e.g., SARIMA) to advanced machine learning and deep learning frameworks for dengue forecasting. For instance, Liu et al. (2025) demonstrated that XGBoost significantly outperformed SARIMA and Support Vector Regression (SVR) in forecasting dengue outbreaks in Bangladesh, primarily due to its ability to capture non-linear relationships with meteorological variables [7]. Similarly, Chen and Moraga (2025) highlighted the efficacy of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and ensemble approaches (LSTM combined with ARIMA or SARIMAX) for weekly dengue forecasting in Rio de Janeiro, noting that ensembles mitigate individual model prediction errors [8]. Furthermore, Sebastianelli et al. (2024) successfully deployed a multi-modal ensemble (CatBoost, SVM, and LSTM) to predict dengue incidence rates across Brazil, proving the value of integrating Earth observation data and socio-economic variables [9].
While these studies establish the superiority of gradient boosting and ensemble methods, they often rely on standard regression objective functions (e.g., RMSE) that may not optimally handle the over-dispersed, count-based nature of epidemiological data. Furthermore, while meteorological lags are commonly used, the integration of biologically-motivated features and granular spatial clustering remains underexplored.
2.	Methodology
2.1 Data Collection and Preprocessing
The foundation of the predictive model relies on a unified dataset comprising daily active dengue cases and localized hotspots. An automated pipeline was developed to ingest, deduplicate, and store this surveillance data into a PostgreSQL database. To ensure spatial accuracy, a bulk reverse-geocoding process utilizing the OpenStreetMap Nominatim API was implemented. This process translates raw geographic coordinates into granular spatial hierarchies, including state, city, district, and localized bounding boxes.

To prepare the data for modeling, a temporal cutoff was applied, and duplicate records were resolved by prioritizing hotspot classifications. Missing meteorological variables (temperature, humidity, and rainfall) were imputed using state-level medians to preserve localized weather patterns. Finally, the target variable, active dengue cases, was log-transformed to stabilize variance and reduce the disproportionate influence of extreme outbreak events during model training [1]. The transformation is defined as:

y′=loge (1+y)

where y represents the raw count of active dengue cases and 
y′ represents the transformed target variable used for model training.
2.2 Feature Engineering
Capturing the complex etiology of dengue transmission requires integrating temporal, spatial, and biological factors. Feature engineering was divided into three primary domains:

Spatial Features: To capture the density and spread of outbreaks, two clustering algorithms were employed. K-Means clustering (k=10) was used to group general geographic locations, while Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (DBSCAN) was applied to differentiate high-density urban transmission zones from rural noise [2]. Additionally, categorical spatial data (e.g., state, city, suburb) were label-encoded.

Meteorological and Biological Features: Mosquito breeding and viral incubation are highly dependent on weather conditions. We generated 7, 14, 21, and 28-day lagged features for temperature, humidity, and rainfall. To capture standing water potential, 14-day and 28-day cumulative rainfall metrics were calculated. Short-term weather trends were smoothed using Exponential Weighted Moving Averages (EWMA). Crucially, a biologically-motivated feature, the Brière thermal performance curve [3], was engineered to model the temperature-dependent development rate of the Aedes aegypti mosquito. The Brière curve is calculated as:

R(T)=c⋅T(T−Tmin )(Tmax−T)1/2

where T is the mean temperature, Tmin and Tmax are the critical minimum and maximum temperatures for mosquito development (set to 13.3 °C and 39.2 °C, respectively), and c is an empirical scaling constant.

Historical Transmission Features: To account for the autoregressive nature of infectious diseases, historical case lags (1, 7, and 30 days) and rolling averages (7 and 30 days) were calculated for each specific geographic centroid.
2.3 Model Architecture
Given the count-based, right-skewed distribution of dengue cases, standard regression techniques are often inadequate. We developed two distinct modeling approaches: a Historical Cases model and a Weather-based model.

The primary architecture proposed is a Stacking Ensemble [4]. The base estimators consist of an XGBoost regressor utilizing a count:poisson objective function and a LightGBM regressor utilizing poisson and tweedie objectives [5, 6]. These specific objective functions are highly suited for modeling over-dispersed count data. For the Poisson regression models, the objective is to minimize the negative log-likelihood loss function:

L(y, ŷ) = (1/N) Σᵢ₌₁ᴺ (ŷᵢ − yᵢ log(ŷᵢ)) 

where yᵢ is the actual count of dengue cases, ŷᵢ is the predicted count, and N is the number of observations. The predictions from these base models are subsequently combined using a Ridge Regression meta-learner to produce the final case prediction.

Additionally, a Two-Stage Hurdle model was evaluated as a comparative baseline. This model first employs a LightGBM classifier to categorize a location as 'normal' or 'outbreak' (defined by a predefined case threshold). Based on this classification, the data is routed to either a low-case LightGBM regressor or a high-case XGBoost regressor. All models were evaluated using a strict time-series split (training on the oldest 80% of data, testing on the newest 20%) to prevent temporal data leakage.

2.4 Methodological Comparison with Existing Frameworks
Our proposed methodology builds upon and refines recent advancements in dengue forecasting. While recent studies, such as those by Chen and Moraga (2025) [8] and Liu et al. (2025) [7], utilize standard meteorological lags (e.g., 1 to 4 weeks) and basic spatial aggregations, our approach introduces two critical methodological enhancements.

Unlike standard approaches that rely purely on raw temperature and humidity data [7, 8], we engineered the Brière thermal performance curve. This non-linear feature explicitly models the temperature-dependent development rate of the Aedes aegypti mosquito, providing the algorithm with a biological context rather than a purely statistical one. Furthermore, while Sebastianelli et al. (2024) [9] utilized Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to capture spatial correlations at the state level, we applied K-Means and DBSCAN clustering directly to reverse-geocoded coordinates. This allows our model to dynamically differentiate between high-density urban transmission zones and rural noise, capturing localized outbreak clusters that state-level aggregations miss.

A common limitation in recent literature is the use of standard regression objectives (like Mean Squared Error) for predicting case counts [7, 9]. Because dengue outbreaks are characterized by extreme, right-skewed peaks (over-dispersion), standard regression often underestimates severe outbreaks. To address this, our Stacking Ensemble utilizes XGBoost with a count:poisson objective and LightGBM with poisson and tweedie objectives. This mathematical formulation is strictly designed for count data, allowing our meta-learner to more accurately predict extreme hotspot events compared to traditional regression ensembles.

3	Results
3.1 Experimental Setup
The models were trained and evaluated using a dataset of 22,534 deduplicated records spanning from December 18, 2025, to March 12, 2026. The dataset exhibited a highly right-skewed distribution of active cases (mean = 3.04, max = 72). To prevent temporal data leakage, the Weather-based model was evaluated using a strict time-series split, training on data prior to February 18, 2026 (18,337 records), and testing on the subsequent period (4,197 records).

3.4 Comparison with Existing Literature
To contextualize the performance of our proposed methodology, we compared our results against recent statistical and machine learning approaches for dengue forecasting across various endemic regions. Direct comparison of absolute error metrics (such as MAE or RMSE) across different geographical regions is challenging due to varying population sizes and baseline incidence rates. However, the Coefficient of Determination (R2), Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE), and structural methodological choices provide a standardized basis for comparison.

Traditional statistical models remain prevalent in the literature but often struggle with non-linear climate interactions. For instance, Withanage et al. (2018) developed a time-series regression model for the Gampaha district in Sri Lanka, achieving an R2 of 0.76 during training but dropping to 0.68 during validation, highlighting the limitations of linear regression in capturing outbreak peaks [10]. Similarly, Kumharn et al. (2023) utilized a Poisson Regression Model (PRM) coupled with Principal Component Analysis to predict dengue in Bangkok, Thailand. While effective at identifying relative humidity as a primary driver, the PRM approach lacked the predictive agility of machine learning ensembles [11]. In Cambodia, Choi et al. (2016) employed negative binomial models to account for over-dispersion in case counts, successfully identifying 0-3 month lags in temperature and rainfall as significant predictors, though the model's predictive accuracy varied heavily by province [12].

Recent literature has increasingly shifted toward advanced machine learning and deep learning frameworks. Kakarla et al. (2023) evaluated multiple models (VAR, GBM, SVR, and LSTM) in Kerala, India, finding that LSTM outperformed traditional models with an R2 of 0.86 and an RMSE of 0.345 [13]. Similarly, Chen and Moraga (2025) highlighted the efficacy of LSTM networks and ensemble approaches (LSTM combined with ARIMA or SARIMAX) for weekly dengue forecasting in Rio de Janeiro, noting that ensembles mitigate individual model prediction errors [8]. Lu et al. (2024) took a hybrid approach in Selangor, Malaysia, coupling a deterministic SI-SIR (Susceptible-Infective-Recovered) model with a statistical ARIMAX model to forecast mosquito biting rates, achieving an average MAPE of 8.01% for a 4-week forecast horizon [14]. Furthermore, Sebastianelli et al. (2024) successfully deployed a multi-modal ensemble (CatBoost, SVM, and LSTM) to predict dengue incidence rates across Brazil, proving the value of integrating Earth observation data and socio-economic variables [9].

While these studies establish the superiority of deep learning and ensemble methods, they often rely on standard regression objective functions or complex neural network architectures that are computationally expensive to train and interpret. Furthermore, while meteorological lags are universally recognized—such as the 2-5 week delays observed by Robert et al. (2023) in the Dominican Republic [15]—the integration of biologically-motivated features and granular spatial clustering into gradient boosting frameworks remains underexplored.

Our proposed Stacking Ensemble (XGBoost + LightGBM) achieved an R2 of 0.9246 when incorporating historical case lags, and a highly competitive R2 of 0.7809 relying strictly on weather and spatial features. This demonstrates that combining count-specific objective functions (Poisson/Tweedie) with advanced spatial clustering (DBSCAN) and biological features (Brière thermal curve) yields state-of-the-art predictive accuracy. By utilizing tree-based ensembles rather than deep neural networks (like LSTM), our model maintains high computational efficiency and interpretability while matching or exceeding the predictive accuracy reported in recent literature [13, 14].

4	Conclusion
This study demonstrates the efficacy of combining advanced spatial feature engineering with gradient-boosting ensembles for localized dengue case prediction. By utilizing a robust data pipeline that integrates reverse geocoding, Brière thermal curves, and weather lags, we successfully captured the complex etiology of dengue transmission. The proposed Stacking Ensemble, utilizing XGBoost and LightGBM with count-specific objective functions, significantly outperformed traditional standalone models, achieving an R2 of 0.9246 when utilizing historical data and 0.7809 using purely meteorological and spatial indicators. 
The practical implications of this research are significant. By accurately forecasting outbreaks based on readily available meteorological and historical case data, this automated framework provides a valuable tool for public health authorities. It enables the proactive identification of dengue hotspots, allowing for the strategic, preemptive deployment of vector control resources (such as targeted fumigation and community awareness campaigns) before an outbreak reaches its peak.
